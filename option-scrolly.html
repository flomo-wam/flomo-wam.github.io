FloMo architecture — a pretrained video backbone flow-matches 3D motion and robot actions.

The thesis

Motion, not pixels.

Pretrained video models carry internet-scale motion priors, but predicting full video is wasteful for action. FloMo fine-tunes a video model to predict dense 3D motion and robot actions together — beating pixel prediction on success, generalization, and sample efficiency.

Step 1 · Inputs

An image and an instruction.

Each rollout starts from a single camera frame and a language command — the only conditioning FloMo needs to begin denoising a plan.

Step 2 · Backbone

A pretrained video model.

A Wan 2.2 5B DiT backbone carries the internet-scale motion prior. We keep its VAE and UMT5 text encoder frozen and fine-tune the trunk with LoRA (rank 64) — no full retraining.

Step 3 · The intermediate

Scene flow rendered as RGB.

FloMo predicts the 3D displacement of each tracked point and renders it as an RGB video. Rendered flow lands in the same latent space as video, so the frozen VAE encodes it with no new tokenizer. Motion shares the video latent.

Step 4 · Two heads

One backbone, two outputs.

The trunk flow-matches both targets jointly: a motion head producing dense 3D scene flow and an action head producing robot actions. At inference it denoises 10 UniPC steps, then executes a chunk of 16 actions before re-planning.

The ownable trick

Every motion vector is one color.

A 3D velocity (ΔX, ΔY, ΔZ) maps linearly to one (R, G, B) value. Motion fields become color fields; zero motion is mid-grey. It is exactly the token the frozen VAE reads — and you can drive the full encoder yourself further down.

The payoff

Predicting motion wins.

On real-robot manipulation FloMo averages 87% in-distribution success — versus 47%, 40%, and 7% for video-predicting and from-scratch baselines. Remove the human-video subset and out-of-distribution success collapses from 80% → 7%.

Abstract

Motion mediates the transfer from video to action

Pretrained video models carry internet-scale motion priors, but predicting full video is wasteful for action. We argue dense 3D motion is how that prior best transfers to action. FloMo is a 5B video model fine-tuned to predict 3D scene flow and actions jointly: scene flow is rendered as RGB and encoded into the video latent space, so no new tokenizer is needed. Co-trained on action-free human video and robot teleoperation, it generalizes to unseen objects, motion primitives, and tasks.

Removing the motion objective hurts generalization; adding a video objective hurts success. Action tokens attend roughly 3× more to motion than to video — motion mediates the transfer from video to action.

Contributions

We render 3D scene flow as RGB and encode it with a frozen video VAE, so motion shares the video latent space. One backbone flow-matches motion and action — no new tokenizer.

2

Motion is the most effective target

On real-robot manipulation, predicting motion beats predicting video — or both — on in-distribution success and OOD generalization. Adding a video target interferes with action learning.

3

Generalization from human video

FloMo transfers to objects, motions, and tasks seen only in action-free human video — like pulling a string, never present in robot training.

Method

3D scene flow as the video-action intermediate

The action that creates a future state depends on how the scene moves, not how it looks. 2D optical flow mixes camera motion with task motion, so FloMo predicts 3D scene flow: the 3D displacement of each tracked point in the first camera frame, isolating object and hand motion.

We render this as an RGB video and encode it with the frozen Wan 2.2 VAE. Rendered flow lands in the same latent space as video, so the pretrained DiT handles it with no new tokenizer — we fine-tune only the backbone (LoRA, rank 64).

Scene-flow tokenization. Each clip plays left→right: raw observation, the recovered one-step 3D motion field as colour-coded arrows, and that flow rendered as an RGB token. The arrows land on the moving hand and object, staying invariant to the texture changes that confuse pixel-space predictors.

The full architecture tap the +

Two-stage co-training

FloMo is co-trained on a mixture of egocentric human video and bimanual robot teleoperation.

Stage 1
Mid-training

Train on the full mixture. Human video supervises scene flow only; robot data supervises scene flow and action — building a broad motion prior at scale.

Stage 2
Fine-tuning

Train on robot data only, dropping human video. This sharpens action prediction without diluting the gradient with action-free samples.

At inference, from an image and a language instruction, FloMo denoises for 10 UniPC steps, then executes a chunk of 16 actions before re-planning.

In-distribution real-robot evaluation

On three contact-rich bimanual tasks (Oven, Toolbox, Corn), FloMo wins every task, averaging 87% — versus 47% for Joint Video-Action, 40% for FloMo w/ Video, and 7% for from-scratch behavior cloning.

The gap is largest on Oven: 3D motion targets the gripper–handle contact directly, while a video target wastes capacity reconstructing reflections and textures.

Out-of-distribution generalization
