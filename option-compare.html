3D Scene Flow as a World-Action Model Intermediate for Learning from Human Video

Most world-action models predict future pixels. FloMo predicts dense 3D motion instead. Motion is the medium through which video pretraining transfers to action.

Architecture

One backbone predicts motion and action

Abstract

Motion mediates the transfer from video to action

Pretrained video models carry internet-scale motion priors, but predicting full video is wasteful for action. We argue dense 3D motion is how that prior best transfers to action. FloMo is a 5B video model fine-tuned to predict 3D scene flow and actions jointly: scene flow is rendered as RGB and encoded into the video latent space, so no new tokenizer is needed. Co-trained on action-free human video and robot teleoperation, it generalizes to unseen objects, motion primitives, and tasks.

Removing the motion objective hurts generalization; adding a video objective hurts success. Action tokens attend roughly 3× more to motion than to video. Motion mediates the transfer from video pretraining to action.

Contributions

What we show

01

Motion as the WAM prediction target

We render 3D scene flow as RGB and encode it with the frozen video VAE, so motion shares the video latent space. A single backbone flow-matches motion and action with no new tokenizer.

02

Motion is the most effective target

On real-robot manipulation, predicting motion gives higher in-distribution success and out-of-distribution generalization than predicting video, or than jointly predicting video and motion. Adding a video target interferes with action learning.

03

Generalization from human video

FloMo transfers to objects, motion primitives, and tasks seen only in action-free human video, such as string-pulling, never present in robot training.

Method

3D scene flow as the video-action intermediate

The action that creates a future state depends on how the scene moves, not how it looks. 2D optical flow mixes camera motion with task motion, so FloMo predicts 3D scene flow: the 3D displacement of each tracked point in the first camera frame, isolating object and hand motion.

We render this as an RGB video and encode it with the frozen Wan 2.2 VAE. Rendered flow lands in the same latent space as video, so the pretrained DiT handles it with no new tokenizer. We fine-tune only the backbone (LoRA, rank 64).

Each clip plays left→right: the raw RGB observation, the recovered one-step 3D motion field as colour-coded arrows, and that flow rendered as an RGB token. The arrows track the moving hand and object, staying invariant to the texture changes that confuse pixel-space predictors.

Two-stage co-training

FloMo is co-trained on a mixture of egocentric human video and bimanual robot teleoperation.

Stage 1
Mid-training

Train on the full mixture. Human video supervises scene flow only; robot data supervises scene flow and action. This builds a broad motion prior at scale.

→
Stage 2
Fine-tuning

Train on robot data only, dropping human video. This sharpens action prediction without diluting the gradient with action-free samples.

At inference, from an image and a language instruction, FloMo denoises for 10 UniPC steps, then executes a chunk of 16 actions before re-planning.
